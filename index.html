Flags and Culture

In today's age, flags are seen throughout many walks of life, such as international relations, sporting events, religious gatherings, and rallies and protests. All of these are different and yet in each one you will see flags being raised, some in defiance while others are held in high regard. With 195 countries in the world, there is a vast number of differences between every one of us, however there is one symbol that traverses almost all societies: the flag. This is because flags represent more than just colors and symbols, they share the country's history, represent its people, and work to unify those under it.

Brief History of Flags

Flags originate from vexilloids, used by military leaders as a visible sign to rally around or point out attacks. Later, when silk was invented in China these vexilloids morphed into banners that were both easier to carry and more visible from a distance. Over time this tradition spread from China to Europe, and by the 12th century morphed from a military symbol, to serving as a way for identifying nobility and their domain. Over the next few centuries cities and guilds would adopt their own flags, with the first modern flag (the Dutch revolutionary Prinsenvlag) appearing by the end of the 16th century. This, along with the abolition of most European monarchies, brought colors and designs to flags that took on ideological and political meaning.
