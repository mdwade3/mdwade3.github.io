Flag Characteristics

Shape

Most national flags are rectangular. The only non-rectangular national flags being Vatican City and Switzerland as theirs are of square shape, and Nepal, which has the only 5-sided flag consisting of two horizontally directed triangles placed vertically on top one another.

Rectangular shaped flags hold prominence due to European imperialism in which European powers spread their influence across the globe, resulting in traditions (such as the rectangular flag) becoming adopted by vastly different cultures.

Proportions

The Proportions of a flag are the ratio of the width to the length. The width of a flag is the vertical measurement, measured along the flags hoist (half of flag nearest to the staff). The length of the flag is measured from the hoist to the fly (the opposite end to the staff, mirrors the hoist).

When flags are placed beside each other in international affairs or large spectacles (i.e. Olympics), to retain uniformity, organizers typically attempt to configure all flags to adopt the same proportions. This can lead to distortion of elements since not all rectangular flags are exactly proportional. Therefore, to fix this, it is customary to standardize all flags to the hoist width allowing proportions to be retained, while the length of flags still varies.

In Europe, many flags are historic armorial banners that date back to the medieval ages and thus, must be described in heraldic terms. However, here we will only be discussing modern flags that are based on simple divisions and charges (objects or figures placed on the field of a flag, typically used for symbolism)rather than the more elaborite historical flags of the past.

Basic Flag Divisions:

  • Horizontally
    • Divided in half along the hoist edge separating the upper and lower portion of the flag.
    • Can be done two or more times to create three or more sections of a flag.
  • Vertically
    • Divided in half along the bottom edge separating the Hoist from the Fly.
    • Can be done two or more times to create three or more sections of a flag.
  • Quarterly
    • Vertically and horizontally divided flag.
    • Forms a cross dividing the upper hoist, lower hoist, upper fly, and lower fly.
  • Checky
    • Division of flag into a checkered pattern.
    • Individual pieces of a checkered field are called checks.
  • Saltire
    • Division of field with diagonal and diagonal left strip forming a cross.
    • Divides flag into four quarters consisting of 4 segments.
  • Gyronny
    • Division that is vertically, horizontally, diagonally, and diagonally left divided, with each apex meeting at the center.
    • Typically consisting of 8 ‐ 12 segments.

Basic Flag Charges:

  • Canton
    • Square/rectangular field covering the upper hoist corner.
    • Typically carried with other charges such as stars (i.e. United States of America)
    • Examples: Hawaii, Australia, Malaysia
  • Pile
    • Triangle that reaches from one end of the flag to the other.
    • Common pile starts from the hoist and comes to a point at the fly edge.
    • Examples: Guyana and American Samoa
  • Cross (different variations)
    • Cross
      • Cross that forms in the center of the flag and whose arms extend to the boundaries
      • Examples: England and Georgia (country)
    • Greek Cross
      • Cross whose arms are of equal length
      • Examples: Switzerland and Greece (in canton)
    • Scandinavian Cross
      • Latin cross displaced horizontally toward the hoist end with the vertical arms located in the hoist portions of flag.
      • Examples: Finland, Iceland, and Denmark
  • Star
    • Typically a five-pointed star; located in the center or various locations throughout a flag.
    • Most popular charge, appearing on over 50 national flags.
    • Examples: United States, Ghana, Vietnam
  • Crescent
    • Waning crescent shaped symbol common in Islamic countries and culture, but this isn't necessarily the case for all countries who depict it.
    • Typically depicted alongside a star (above).
    • Examples: Tunisia, Singapore, Pakistan
  • Disc
    • Circular device of a singular color
    • Examples: Japan, Palau, Bangladesh

Colors

Single colors and color combinations are the main way a flag conveys its symbolic meaning. Colors can be displayed to resemble political factions, religious ideologies, national heritage, geographical regions, or hold heraldic meaning. There are many colors used in a plethora of flags throughout the world, with each color holding a different, meaningful context for their respective nation (i.e. The red in the Vietnam's flag stands for the communist revolution, while in Spain's, it is a heraldic remembrance of some of the oldest Spanish kingdoms).

Flag types

Flag Types According to Function:

Civil Flag

Flag designating national identity, flown by private citizens on land. Version of the national flag and can also denote building or ships not crewed by the military.

National Flag

Flag that represents the given nation, including both its citizens and military. Flown by both the government and its citizens within the country. It can be the flag of a nation-state, formerly independent state, or non-independent national group that has its own government. In the case of an independent state, it acts as a flag and ensign (maritime flag for national identification) used by government officials, navy, and general public.

State Flag

Flag flown by government institutions and authorities on land. Used to help designate national identity.

Flag Types According to Usage:

Banner

Used generally to distinguish a square or rectangular flag fastened to a staff or crossbar with an elaborate design. It is often hand-painted and/or embroidered. Typically, hung vertically with the upper hoist still facing in the upper left-hand corner when viewed straight on.

Outdoor Flag

Flag made of stronger fabric or material to survive the outdoor conditions such as strong winds and inclement weather. Has enforced edges and corners, suitable for hoisting on flagstaffs.

Jack

Small flag flown from a jackstaff located in the bow (front of ship) of a warship. Typically hoisted by ships that are harbored or anchored in the roadstead (sheltered water near shore). Typically depicted in the form of a smaller version of one's national flag, but can also be of historical context or special design.

Flag Types according to shape:

Standard

A standard flag can take on one of four meanings. First, it can be a vexilloid (serves same purpose as flag with slightly different appearance) used by the army. Second, a long tapering flag of heraldic design, with its size corresponding to the person's rank. Third, a rectangular flag seen typically in national flags. Finally, a flag used to represent the head of state.

Pennant

Long narrow flags that are are triangular or tapering in shape. Because of this, they are larger at the hoist than at the fly typically coming to point or narrowing the further it extends from the flagpole. Originally used at sea, however in modern day, are used as awards and souvenirs for sports and other organizations.
