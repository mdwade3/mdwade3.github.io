Flag of Oklahoma

Creation History

Oklahoma gained statehood on November 16, 1907, and by 1911 had adopted their first flag. The Original Oklahoma flag was designed by Ruth Clement and incorporated a red backdrop with a white star highlighted in the center. The star was trimmed in blue and featured the number 46 in its center, signifying Oklahoma's admittance as the 46th state.

However, later in the early parts of the 20th century, due to the fear of communism, which was spearheaded by events such as the Russian October revolution of 1917 and anarchist bombings in the US (known as the first Red Scare), a decision to change the Oklahoma flag was made. This was due to Oklahoma's flag consisting of an all-red backdrop, a color closely associated with the communist political movement. A contest was held by the Daughters of the American Revolution, with Louise Fluke designing the winning entry, consisting of an Osage war shield crossed with a calumet and olive tree branch, centered over a field of blue. This design holds true to this day with the only slight change occurring in 1941, when Oklahoma was added in white, Eurostile typeface as an initiative to combat illiteracy in the state.

Features

Oklahoma has deep-seated roots in the history and heritage of Native Americans (some good and some bad), which is depicted in the state's flag. The state is one of only two US flags to portray Native American imagery, illustrated by an Osage war shield and calumet pipe.

The Osage shield is a nod to an actual 1890's bison rawhide Osage war shield belonging to chief Black Dog II, who was a company commander in the Osage Battalion, a Native American military unit of the Confederate States Army. This element pays homage to the Native American heritage of the state, honoring its indigenous roots. Together with the calumet and olive branch, the three combine to represent defensive warfare and protection.

On the shield, there are 6 horizontally aligned crosses depicted inside the shield and 7 feathers of which hang from its bottom margin. The six crosses are meant to resemble the Native American configuration of the stars. They symbolize high ideals, holding the people within the state to standards of honesty and integrity in all facets of life. The seven feathers are a nod to the many tribes who inhabit the state of Oklahoma, specifically recognizing the Choctaw, Chickasaw, Cherokee, Creek, Seminole, Quapaw, and Osage tribes.

The calumet (peace pipe) and olive branch are superimposed in a diagonal cross over the Osage shield and have a combined meaning when depicted beside each other. By themselves, the calumet, a sacred object in many Native tribes used in a variety of ceremonies for prayer or sealing of a treaty (typically associated with peace), denotes the Indigenous People who inhabit the state. While the olive branch corresponds to European settlers and is a classical representation of peace in western society. Although the two societies have had varying conflicts throughout America's history, such as the American Indian war and the Trail of Tears (the latter being the forced relocation of many Indigenous tribes to the Oklahoma territory, attributing to its high Native American population), the calumet (Native American's) and olive branch (European settlers) are the symbolized desire for peace between the two communities, as each stands for tranquility within their respective cultures.

The field of French blue in which the war shield, calumet, and olive branch reside, is a credit to the first official Native American flag flown in Oklahoma, the Choctaw battle flag. This flag was raised by the men of the Second Indian Brigade, a native American military troop in the confederate army of the American Civil War. The color also is meant to represent devotion and loyalty, emphasizing unity amongst all the people of Oklahoma. Also depicted atop the blue background, in the bottom portion of the flag is the word Oklahoma in white lettering. This was placed on the flag to combat rising illiteracy rates within the state and highlight the state to which the flag belonged.
