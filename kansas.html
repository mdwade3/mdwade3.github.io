Flag of Kansas

Creation History

While Kansas was admitted to the Union on January 29, 1861, it did not adopt an official state flag until March 21,1927. The process for acquiring a state flag began in 1916 when a flag design contest was held by the Daughters of the American Revolution (a federally chartered organization for women descended from patriots of the American Revolution). The winner was Esther Nortrup whose design consisted of three red, white, and blue horizontal strips and a depiction of the state seal surrounded by a sunflower in the canton (upper left). This flag was submitted to legislator but ultimately did not pass due to pressures from organizations such as the Grand Army of the Republic, who said any state flag would combat the US national flag and defy unity amongst the nation, while others simply thought of the sunflower as a weed that should not represent the state.

Sometime later a banner would be created by Albert T. Reid, depicting a sunflower and the state seal, centered in a field of blue, and draped with golden fringes. This satisfied the Grand Army of the Republic, but groups such as the Daughters of the American Revolution and the Kansas National Guard were left unappeased. To make matters worse, the display of the Kansas state banner was rejected for display among the state flags in Washington, leaving Kansas with no physical state representation in the nation's capital.

It wasn't until General Milton R. Mclean pushed for Kansas to adopt an official state flag that any headway was gained toward the resolution on the matter. Ultimately, a design by Hazel Avery that included a field of blue with the state seal in the center and a sunflower on top was passed by legislation on March 21, 1921, finally after nearly 60 years of statehood giving Kansas its first official state flag. In 1961, the addition of the word Kansas was added in gold block lettering at the bottom of the flag, and it hasn't changed since.

Features

The Kansas state flag depicts a sunflower on top a red and blue stripped wreath with the state seal in the center and the word Kansas in gold block lettering engulfed in blue backdrop. The Sunflower is one of the most common state symbols of Kansas, representing both the state and its people. The flower even takes hold in the states nickname the Sunflower state due to the abundance of Wild Native Sunflowers throughout Kansas. Beneath the sunflower, is a blue and yellow striped wreath meant to represent an 1803 land treaty, known as the Louisiana Purchase. This purchase would mark a key moment in US history as it would act as the catalyst for the great western expansion and manifest destiny as American's flocked to settle the west and its territories for the opportunities they believed were provided. The Kansas territory was included in this purchase, resulting in many new settlers in the early parts of the 19th century.

The Kansas state seal includes many elements that ultimately tell the story of Kansas's past and history. The seal, created by senator John Ingalls in 1861, includes the phrase (and state motto) Ad astra per aspera meaning To the Stars through Difficulties signifying the political struggles the state endured in the leadup to its admission as a free state in 1961. This period was known as Bleeding Kansas and resulted from the dispute over legality of slavery in the territory, leading to violent civil conflicts between 1854-1861. Kansas's eventual admission as free state was approved by the House of Representatives but had been blocked by southern senators until 1961 when Abraham Lincoln was elected president, paving the way for Kansas to achieve statehood as the 34th state, depicted by the 34 stars on the flag in the upper portion of the state seal.

The rising eastward sun, along with the wagon train and log cabin pays homage to the settlers that took the opportunity to relocate to the new lands of the Kansas territory. The rising sun signifies the east and how the settlement of the west marked a new beginning in American history where those who sought the opportunity of a new life could find it in the uncharted lands to the west. The wagon train in combatant with the state's motto (see above) has the added meaning of signifying the difficulties western settlers endured in the trek for a better life.

The bison is the state's animal, yet another symbol of the state of Kansas. In the seal, Native American's are shown hunting a heard of buffalo, a common pastime of the Plains Indian people. These hunting parties were a fundamental activity that supplied meat and fur for both their society and economy, up until the animals near extinction due to the US's expansion of the west. For the Plains tribes, bison hunting also acted as a form of spiritual and religious practice due to the buffalo's perception as a sacred animal amongst their people.

The steamboat, river, and ploughing of the land, together represent the state's overall infrastructure and flow of commerce. The horses ploughing the land in the foreground acknowledge agriculture as historically Kansas's most prominent industry, Producing a significant amount of the US's grain, corn, and meat (beef). These products are then transported along waterways, such as the Kansas and Missouri river, (depicted by the steamboat and river), both of which act as tributaries to the Mississippi river, America's most prominent river and supplier of commerce.
