Flag of Arizona

Creation History

Arizona's flag was created by Colonel Charles Wilfred Harris of the Arizona National Guard to serve as a representation of the national guard's rifle team during competitions. Initially, legislation failed to pass in 1915, which would have made it the official state flag of Arizona, due to similarities with Japan's current flag. However, the design would eventually pass, and on February 27, 1917, was adopted as the official state flag of Arizona and has been the flag ever since.

Features

Arizona's flag consists of alternating red and yellow sun beams displayed horizontally along the top half, with a liberty blue colored foreground, and a copper star in the center. The alternating red and yellow sunbeams have three simultaneous meanings hidden withing them. First, the two colors (red and yellow) are a tribute to some of the earliest explorers of the Arizona region during the mid sixteenth century. These Spanish explores, known as conquistadores, were headed by Francisco Vazquez de Coronado and are credited with traversing much of the present-day southwestern United States and Mexico with the hopes of finding the seven cities of gold, discovering landmarks such as the Grand Canyon along the way. The sunbeams second meaning is in the number of alternating stripes (sunbeams) within the design. There are thirteen stripes in total which are salute to the original thirteen colonies that receded from British rule in 1776, therefore, joining together to establish the United States of America. Lastly, the sunbeams are a representation of a westward setting sun, signifying Arizona's geographic location in the southwestern region of the US.

The Liberty Blue color depicted in the bottom half of the Arizona flag is another nod to the country in which it belongs, as it is the same hue as seen on the national flag of the United States. It also is meant to resemble a principal river of the southwest, the Colorado river. This river is known for carving the Grand Canyon, a landmark that is one of the seven natural wonders of the world and Arizona's largest tourist attraction. It also supplies the water necessary to operate the Hoover Dam, generating power for utilities in Arizona, Nevada, and California.

Finally, the copper star, depicted in the center, credits the importance of copper mining within Arizona's infrastructure. Historically, the mining industry has played an important role in Arizona's economy, and copper has been its most abundant resource and chief export, accounting for 65% of all US copper.
